_____Math is often called the universal language - a set of rules and symbols that transcend cultures and civilizations
But what if we encountered an alien race that didn't follow the same mathematical conventions as us?
How would that affect their understanding of the universe?

Imagine there's a super advanced alien civilization out there, light-years away.
They might be brilliant mathematicians, but what if they solve equations in a completely different order than we do?
Like, they multiply before they add, or something crazy like that!

We realize their approach to basic arithmetic is...different.
Instead of following the order of operations we know as BODMAS or PEMDAS on Earth, their order is completely scrambled.
What impact might that have?

Would that mean they'd always get different answers than us?
Would all their scientific theories and calculations be totally messed up?
Or is there some kind of universal math truth everyone ends up with, no matter the order they solve things in?

Not necessarily! While the order of operations is an agreed-upon convention that allows us to interpret equations consistently,
it doesn't change the underlying mathematical truths.
The fundamental principles of mathematics are based on logic and reason - they should remain constant across civilizations.

If the alien civilization prioritizes operations like addition before multiplication,
their solutions to equations like "2 + 3 x 4" would certainly diverge from ours.
But that doesn't mean either of our mathematics is inherently wrong or unequal. It simply reflects different conventions for notation and symbolic interpretation.

Mathematics describes the relationships and patterns governing our universe.
While the specific symbolic representations may vary,
the essential mathematical laws and models aligning with observable reality should remain universal across cultures.
The key would be establishing a common lexicon - understanding each other's notation systems and conventions - to ensure our theoretical calculations produce equivalent results.
Otherwise, we might be speaking the same language of mathematics but with different dialects!
Just like we wouldn't claim one human language is superior to another,
no single convention for mathematical notation is more valid. The crucial hallmark is whether it consistently maps to the empirical truths of our cosmos.

So while alien mathematics might look different on the surface,
the underlying logic and first principles governing the language of the universe should still be inharmonious equilibrium, waiting to be decoded.
